# Stress Engineering: Calculating Comparative Stress and Tensile Strength of Paper Materials

## Overview
This project investigates the mechanical behaviour of various paper-based materials 
by calculating their comparative stress and tensile strength under applied loads. 
Conducted as a physics personal project, it explores how material thickness, 
composition, and internal structure influence a material's ability to withstand force 
— core concepts in materials science and mechanical engineering.

## Research Question
How does the thickness and material composition of paper-based materials affect 
their comparative stress and tensile strength?

## Materials Tested
- A4 sheet (single, double, triple, quadruple layers)
- Origami paper
- Sticky notes
- Newspaper
- Sandpaper
- Thicker A4 sheet
- Tissue paper
- Notebook paper

## Methodology
A standardised tensile test was designed using basic apparatus — slotted weights, 
string, a paper cup, and a Vernier caliper for precise thickness measurement. 
Each material sample was suspended from a table edge and incrementally loaded 
until failure.

Two key mechanical properties were calculated:

**Comparative Stress** = Weight (g) ÷ Thickness (cm) — measures load supported 
per unit thickness

**Tensile Strength** = Force (N) ÷ Cross-Sectional Area (cm²) — standard measure 
of maximum force per unit area before failure

## Key Findings
- Strong positive correlation (r = 0.97) between material thickness and weight held
- Thinner materials like newspaper and origami paper showed surprisingly high 
comparative stress values — attributed to dense cellulose fibre composition and 
surface treatment
- Layering A4 sheets produced a near-linear increase in both thickness and 
load-bearing capacity
- Material composition and internal structure, not just thickness, are the dominant 
factors in tensile performance

## Data & Visualisation
The project includes:
- Full tabular data with Vernier caliper measurements
- Bar graph of weight-to-thickness ratios across all materials
- Scatter plot with linear regression line (y = 4516x + 42) showing thickness vs 
weight relationship
- Polynomial regression analysis for layered A4 sheets

## Relevance to Materials Science
This experiment demonstrates fundamental materials science principles — 
specifically how microstructure and composition govern mechanical properties 
independent of macroscopic dimensions. The finding that thinner materials can 
outperform thicker ones under stress directly challenges intuitive assumptions and 
reflects real-world materials selection challenges in packaging, aerospace, and 
structural engineering.
